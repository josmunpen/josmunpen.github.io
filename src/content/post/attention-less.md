Attention is all you need was a turning point. Attention mechanisms that made coherent language generation and understanding possible for the first time — networks that could actually understand the relevance of a token within its context.

Funny that those same mechanisms might be causing attention problems in humans.

Who would have thought that, 9 years later, I'd be wondering whether transformers cause attention problems. But a different kind. You have a task. You sketch a solution or ask a coding agent for a plan. You make some adjustments, say "Let's go." And in that moment, your attention flies. It knows it has a few seconds or minutes with no serious cognitive work required, and it relaxes automatically.
In my case, that usually means checking my chats, opening email, reviewing pending tasks.

Some gurus argue that a developer should work with n agents — n being the number of parallel tasks needed to force you to become the bottleneck, meaning the agents are the ones waiting on you.

I've been trying different approaches. One: launching 5 tasks in parallel, then reviewing them in round-robin order — task 1, task 2, task 3, 4, 5, repeat.

For me this isn't cognitively natural. Each round I barely have time to recover context for anything with a minimum of complexity, and knowing that 3 agents are already waiting ends up making me rush and not pay the attention things deserve.

Another approach: take one task, and while the coding agent works, either follow its thinking and tool executions obsessively — not the friendliest trace to read — or proactively analyze the involved code in parallel.

Two problems with this: one, it doesn't get the most out of parallelizing async work with agents. Two, it's a cognitive effort that's hard to commit to when you have a very comfortable alternative — don't do it, wait, check your phone, open email.

I don't have a clear solution. The only thing that's working for me — at least enough not to leave my brain wrecked — is keeping my phone out of the room and working in 20-minute focus sessions. During those 20 minutes: no song changes, no unlocking the phone, no replying to Teams. I try to work with 1 or 2 agents in parallel depending on task complexity, but I haven't managed to push the async value higher without it hurting my focus — and therefore the work I deliver.

Attention looks expensive. Just not in the way we thought.
